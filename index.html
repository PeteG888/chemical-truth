H2S: 4.2ppm TEMP: 847°C PRESS: 142bar DAY: 47/60 THE ENGINEER EPISODE 01 — THE CHEMICAL TRUTH The Smell Nobody Talks About NARRATOR Day 47 of the shutdown… and the heat exchanger bundle smelled like something that had been slowly expiring inside a chemical drum for the better part of a decade. Which was, as it happened… a reasonably accurate description of its contents. Ren had worked enough turnarounds to know the smell without needing the label. MEK. Methyl ethyl ketone… fast… aggressive… on the approved chemical register since before anyone currently on site had started shaving. The circuit had a leak. Not a dramatic one. Just a slow… patient seep from a flange coupling on the return line. The kind that caught the light… and then evaporated before anyone thought to measure it. Ren had logged it on Day 44. Someone had initialled the log on Day 45… without leaving their office to confirm it. REN Del was on the far side of the bundle, torquing the bolts on a blind flange cover. Methodical. Careful. This morning… he had torqued the same bolt three times. NARRATOR The LEL monitor on Ren's belt read 8.4%. The action threshold was 10%. What the LEL monitor did not measure… was health exposure. 8.4% translated to approximately 1,176 parts per million in the air they were breathing. The legal limit was 200. The monitor was doing its job perfectly. Its job was to prevent an explosion. Nobody's job, apparently… was to eliminate the health exposure. SETCH We're behind on the bundle return schedule. Get it done. REN I gave the universal gesture that meant I'd handle it. NARRATOR What Ren did not say… was that Del had mentioned headaches every morning for eleven days. That two other crew members had visited the site medic so often the medic had filed an informal query. The MEK safety data sheet listed persistent headache, dizziness, and impaired cognitive function under repeated exposure. Repeated exposure was doing extraordinary work in a confined space with a leaking return line on Day 47 of a sixty-day turnaround. DEL I set the torque wrench down… stood very still for a moment… then picked it up and kept going. NARRATOR The LEL monitor ticked to 8.6%. ■ To Be Continued ■ The LEL monitor ticked to 8.6%. The entry permit was clipped to the access ladder. Ren reached for it — and stopped. The atmospheric reading recorded at 06:00 that morning was 4.1%. It was now 09:23. The LEL was climbing, but the paperwork said everything was fine. Ren looked at the signature in the authorising officer field. It was his own handwriting. Precise. Familiar. But he had no memory of holding the pen. No memory of the 06:00 check at all. The fog wasn't just in the heat exchanger anymore… it was in his head.
